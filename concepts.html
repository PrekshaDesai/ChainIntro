Web3 Concepts Simplified

Compare fundamental blockchain ideas side-by-side to understand why they differ and how the technology is structured.

Web2

Centralized Platform Control

Web2 is the read-write web governed by centralized platforms (like Google, Amazon, or Meta). Users get "free" services in exchange for yielding control of their private behavioral data and content. Digital identities and profiles remain captive in corporate databases, subject to platform policies.

Web3

Decentralized User Ownership

Web3 is the read-write-own web powered by public blockchains. Users interact peer-to-peer using self-sovereign digital assets and wallets without middle-men. Instead of renting accounts from tech corporations, users own their data, domain names, and digital credentials directly on-chain.

Bitcoin

Decentralized Store of Value

Bitcoin was created in 2009 as a peer-to-peer electronic cash system. Today, it acts primarily as "digital gold" — a highly secure, scarce store of value with a strict supply cap of 21 million coins. It operates on a simple, robust code layout designed specifically for secure transactional ledgers.

Ethereum

Programmable World Computer

Ethereum takes blockchain technology and adds a Turing-complete smart contract execution environment. It acts as a global computer where developers write automated, code-based protocols (dApps). It hosts decentralized finance (DeFi), gaming, organizational governance (DAOs), and NFTs.

Public Key

Your Wallet Mailing Address

Your public key is a cryptographic sequence that defines your location on the blockchain. It behaves like an email address or a bank routing number — it is completely safe to share with anyone who wants to send you digital assets or audit your public ledger transactions.

Private Key

Your Vault Password & Signature

Your private key is a secret code that serves as your mathematical signature to unlock and authorize transfers. If a third party gains access to your private key, they can steal your entire wallet instantly. It must never be shared, as it is the final authority of account ownership.

Database

Centralized Admin & CRUD Operations

Traditional databases are owned by one entity that appoints administrators. Admins possess Create, Read, Update, and Delete (CRUD) permissions over all records. This centralized structure is extremely fast and efficient, but it requires users to trust the hosting entity not to modify or delete logs.

Blockchain

Append-Only Immutable Ledger

Blockchains are decentralized write-once, read-many ledgers maintained by a consensus of nodes. Once a block of transactions is cryptographically stamped and added to the chain, it becomes permanent and cannot be modified or deleted. This immutability builds trust without needing a central coordinator.
